The Regional Languages Of India: Dying A Slow Death?

- By Armaan Sidhu
January 10,2022

The Regional Languages Of India: Dying A Slow Death?
On this World Hindi diwas, warm wishes to all the readers, however it is to be re-informed that the topic is not a sensationalized version of some random conspiracy. Today, check once the uniform resource locators (URLs) of various websites, usernames on Social Media platforms, default language in call centers, main language used for restaurant menus, etc, you’ll find one similarity, everything is only and only in Roman Script - you’ll find latin alphabets everywhere and you can’t use letters from Devanagari script in these cases. This is a clear indication of language dictatorship.

Image Source : Trak.in

In a bold move by the BJP government, on the first anniversary of New Education Policy 2020 (NEP) which laid emphasis on regional languages rather than English, the All India Council for Technical Education (AICTE) chairman Anil Sahasrabudhe announced that AICTE has created a database to promote higher education in regional languages. In November this year, PM Modi launched Engineering Courses in 5 local languages in 13 colleges available this academic year. However, he announced that scientific words will not be translated to hold the main crux of the course without losing some part of it in the process.
Today, English, French, Spanish are considered ‘global languages’, and on the other hand Chinese, Hindi, Arabic - even though having hundreds of millions of speakers are considered ‘local languages’. This tendency is a result of years of colonization and years of colonial hangover afterwards which continue even to the date. Today India, the Arab world, and Africa face the same language problem. With an increase in Population and rapid urbanization, youth now require these ‘global languages’ at the workplace. But the issue is mixing of global and local languages of which as a result the local language often gets killed or the vocabulary of it gets shrinked. Today if someone is not able to understand English in India, he is considered an idiot which is the output of the long awaited Colonial hangover.
While the Chinese, Japanese and Koreans have effectively balanced between their ‘local language’ and English - such that it is not considered a point of shame to not know English or to use their mother tongue while talking at home or with friends.
As a result of English Dictatorship in India, An average Indian might think that he is conversing with his friends in Hindi, but the reality is that no one speaks even a full sentence in Hindi nowadays, the only Language used is Hinglish. Real Hindi as a language is dying a slow death and if not revived soon, it might go extinct like we see today. Hindi's vocabulary is shrinked than it was before. People felt shame in conversing in Hindi and substituted it’s words with English which resulted in those words eventually being forgotten and deleted out of their Hindi Vocabulary.

Today, whenever we translate sentences to Hindi, the English word is simply written with Hindi alphabets because the majority of people don’t remember their substitutes and the language is decayed and eventually lost.
As with the Arab Countries and Africa, students face a new problem - they spoke a language thousands of years (or more) old, and a language rich of culture and heritage but they are consistently being forced by parents to learn English (in Arab Countries) and French (in Africa) which will make them work more effectively at workplace. However the UAE government deserves appreciation in this regard. In UAE, any sign board or printed information (like printed information on eatables) has to be compulsorily in Arabic along with English. This has been done to preserve their language and make it at-par with the so-called ‘global language’ English.

A Coca-Cola bottle in UAE
(Coca-Cola written in Arabic with English version written on backside of the bottle)
The BJP government should do the same in India. All information printed should be in English as well as the state’s most spoken official language.
Today India has many External problems like - China, Pakistan, Afghanistan, Myanmar and Internal as well - Terrorism in J&K, Suicide Bombings, North East Insurgency, Attempts for revival of Khalistan Movement etc. however the issue with these is that our common man can play only a little or no role in curbing out these, mostly he can do only one thing to try to make himself better economically so that eventually our country’s collective GDP and PCI Increases, however in this list of Internal Issues, we add one more today - Slow Death of Regional Languages.
The fact is that the common man can play an extended role in curbing this problem.
Actually the government plays little role in it, the common man has the large part of responsibility - converse in your mother tongue in online chats, try to minimize the use of Hinglish and lay emphasis on real Hindi, ignore those who laugh at you while you use Hindi. And the most important - take pride in Hindi, never consider it a ‘local language’ or inferior to the so-called ‘global languages’. Hindi speakers today are over 500 Million. If we start collective initiatives, in the future we will see foreigners learning Hindi (Already many are learning Hindi nowadays), Use of Hindi sign boards in main International Airports (Already this has been done in Dubai International Airport for Arabic language).
So it will take a collective effort from the government and people to protect our culture, heritage, language or else we would lose it to the so-called ‘global language’ English.
